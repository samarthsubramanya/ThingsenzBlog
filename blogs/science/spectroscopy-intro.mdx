## What Is Spectroscopy?

Spectroscopy is the study of how matter **interacts with electromagnetic radiation**. By measuring which wavelengths of light are absorbed, emitted, or scattered by a substance, chemists can determine its identity, structure, and concentration — often without destroying the sample.

The core principle is simple: every element and molecule has a unique "fingerprint" in the electromagnetic spectrum.

## The Electromagnetic Spectrum

Light comes in many forms, and different regions probe different molecular properties:

| Region | Wavelength Range | What It Probes |
|---|---|---|
| X-ray | 0.01 – 10 nm | Core electron transitions |
| UV-Visible | 200 – 800 nm | Valence electron transitions |
| Infrared (IR) | 2.5 – 25 μm | Molecular vibrations & bonds |
| Microwave | 1 mm – 1 m | Molecular rotations |
| NMR (radio) | ~1 m | Nuclear spin states |

## Beer-Lambert Law

The foundation of quantitative UV-Vis spectroscopy is the **Beer-Lambert Law**:

> A = ε · c · l

Where:
- **A** = absorbance (dimensionless)
- **ε** = molar absorptivity (L mol⁻¹ cm⁻¹)
- **c** = concentration (mol L⁻¹)
- **l** = path length (cm)

This linear relationship lets you calculate concentration directly from measured absorbance — the basis of countless analytical assays.

## Infrared Spectroscopy

IR spectroscopy identifies **functional groups** by their characteristic bond vibration frequencies. A carbonyl (C=O) stretches near 1700 cm⁻¹; an O-H broad stretch appears around 3200–3550 cm⁻¹.

A simple Python snippet to visualize a synthetic IR spectrum:

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Wavenumber axis (cm⁻¹)
wavenumbers = np.linspace(4000, 400, 1000)

def lorentzian(x, x0, gamma, A):
    return A * (gamma**2 / ((x - x0)**2 + gamma**2))

# Carbonyl C=O stretch ~1715 cm⁻¹
spectrum = lorentzian(wavenumbers, 1715, 20, 0.9)
# O-H stretch ~3300 cm⁻¹
spectrum += lorentzian(wavenumbers, 3300, 200, 0.7)
# C-H stretch ~2950 cm⁻¹
spectrum += lorentzian(wavenumbers, 2950, 30, 0.4)

# Convert to transmittance
transmittance = 1 - np.clip(spectrum, 0, 1)

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 4))
plt.plot(wavenumbers, transmittance * 100, color='#388bfd', linewidth=1.5)
plt.gca().invert_xaxis()
plt.xlabel('Wavenumber (cm⁻¹)')
plt.ylabel('Transmittance (%)')
plt.title('Simulated IR Spectrum of a Carboxylic Acid')
plt.grid(alpha=0.2)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
```

## NMR Spectroscopy

Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) is arguably the most powerful structural tool in chemistry. Protons (¹H) in different chemical environments resonate at slightly different **chemical shifts** (δ, in ppm).

Key reference points for ¹H NMR:
- **TMS (tetramethylsilane)** — reference standard at 0 ppm
- **Alkyl CH₃** — ~0.9 ppm
- **Aromatic H** — 7.0–8.5 ppm
- **Aldehyde CHO** — 9–10 ppm
- **Carboxylic COOH** — 10–12 ppm

## Astronomical Applications

Spectroscopy is not limited to the lab. The **absorption lines** in stellar spectra reveal the chemical composition of stars millions of light-years away. The discovery of helium itself came first from solar spectroscopy in 1868 — before it was ever found on Earth.

The same Beer-Lambert principles that govern a 1 cm cuvette apply to light traveling across the galaxy.
