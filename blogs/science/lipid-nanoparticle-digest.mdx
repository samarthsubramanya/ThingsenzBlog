## What This Post Covers

This digest pulls together three papers published between 2022 and 2024, all circling the same question: **why have lipid nanoparticles (LNPs) become the dominant delivery system for nucleic acid therapies?** We'll skip the jargon wherever possible.

---

## Background in 30 Seconds

Your body's cells are surrounded by a fatty membrane. If you want to deliver a therapeutic molecule — say, an mRNA strand that teaches immune cells to fight a tumour — you need something that can:

1. Protect the cargo from enzymes in the bloodstream that would destroy it within minutes
2. Fuse with the target cell's membrane and release the cargo inside
3. Not trigger a dangerous immune reaction in the process

Lipid nanoparticles (tiny spherical fat droplets, ~100 nm across) tick all three boxes. The mRNA COVID-19 vaccines were the first mass-scale proof.

---

## Paper 1 — The Ionisable Lipid Is the Key Ingredient

**Cited work:** Eygeris *et al.*, *Accounts of Chemical Research*, 2022.

The authors did a systematic sweep of LNP formulations and landed on one clear finding: the **ionisable lipid component** — which carries a positive charge at low pH but is neutral at body pH — is the single biggest determinant of delivery efficiency.

Why does this matter? At the slightly acidic pH inside an endosome (the cell's intake pouch), the ionisable lipid goes positive, disrupts the endosomal membrane, and releases mRNA into the cytoplasm. At normal body pH 7.4, it stays neutral and doesn't provoke immune recognition.

The practical takeaway: swapping just this one lipid component can change cellular uptake by **10–100×**, which explains why so much pharmaceutical R&D is focused on finding better ionisable lipid structures.

## Paper 2 — Targeting Organs Beyond the Liver

**Cited work:** Cheng *et al.*, *Nature Nanotechnology*, 2023.

The default destination for intravenously injected LNPs is the liver — useful for liver diseases, but a limitation for everything else. This paper introduced **selective organ targeting (SORT)** by adding a fifth lipid component whose charge ratio steers LNPs toward the lungs, spleen, or liver on demand.

Key results:

| SORT lipid % (cationic) | Primary target organ |
|---|---|
| 0 % | Liver |
| ~7.5 % | Spleen |
| ~50 % | Lungs |

For cancer immunotherapy this is significant: the spleen is packed with immune cells (T cells, B cells, dendritic cells) that need to be primed to attack tumours. Being able to dial in splenic delivery without surgery opens up new treatment design space.

---

## Paper 3 — LNPs That Load Combination Therapies

**Cited work:** Rosenblum *et al.*, *Nature Communications*, 2024.

Single-agent therapies rarely cure solid tumours — resistance develops. This group co-encapsulated **mRNA encoding a tumour antigen** (to trigger immunity) with **siRNA silencing an immune checkpoint gene** (to stop the tumour from switching off the immune attack) in the same LNP.

In mouse models of melanoma, the dual-payload approach produced complete tumour regression in a significant fraction of animals, compared to near-zero for either payload alone.

The engineering challenge they solved: the two RNA types have different sizes and charge densities, so they compete for space inside the LNP. The team tuned lipid ratios and pH during assembly to co-encapsulate both at therapeutically relevant doses without one crowding out the other.

---

## What This Adds Up To

These three papers, read together, sketch a progression:

- **How to make LNPs work** (ionisable lipid chemistry)
- **Where to send them** (SORT targeting)
- **What to put inside** (combination payloads)

The implication for drug development is that LNPs are now less of a fixed platform and more of a **programmable delivery system** — tunable for organ, cell type, and cargo combination. Clinical trials for LNP-delivered cancer vaccines are already in Phase II/III as of 2024, which makes the engineering described above directly relevant to therapies likely to reach patients within the next five years.
